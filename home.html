The ε (epsilon) you chose controlled two things at once: how clearly you could see each tile, and how fast your Privacy Budget drained.

In real life, every time a system answers a question about private data it spends some ε. When the budget is gone, it stops — to protect people's secrets. That's differential privacy.

Pearson Correlation — what the variables mean

r  =  Σ (rawi − raw̄)(dpi − dp̄)  /  √[ Σ(rawi − raw̄)² · Σ(dpi − dp̄)² ]
rawi The real, original value for person i — the actual data before any privacy noise is added. Think of it as the true answer.
raw̄ The average of all real values — add up every rawi and divide by the count. It's the "center" of the original data.
dpi The noisy (private) version of person i's value — the original value with random noise added by the DP mechanism to protect privacy.
dp̄ The average of all noisy values — same idea as raw̄ but calculated on the DP data. High ε → dp̄ stays close to raw̄. Low ε → dp̄ drifts further away.
